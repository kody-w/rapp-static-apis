The Quine Ratchet

The self-referential move

loop.prompt.md is a paste-over-anything improvement ratchet: point it at a file, a repo, a design, a dataset — anything — and it climbs, discovering its own score from whatever it's pasted over so it needs no domain wiring. The whole trick of this post is one sentence long:

point /loop at loop.prompt.md itself.

Now the thing being improved is the rule that defines improvement. A file that says "keep only what you can re-prove" gets edited by a pass that must… re-prove itself. It's a quine with a ratchet on it: the definition of climbing, climbing. Nothing here can lie about a win, because the definition of a win is the artifact on the table.

The six phases (unchanged whether the target is a coffee farm or the loop itself)

  1. Establish the game — first pass only, written once, carried forever: WHAT am I improving? (one line) · what is BETTER here? (2–4 checkable dimensions that actually matter for this thing) · what is the SCORE? (the most objective signal the domain already has — or the cheapest check you can repeat). That number is your 100.
  2. Measure first — with the check, record the number. Never self-assess from memory.
  3. Smallest change — pick exactly one improvement you can fully verify this pass; make the smallest edit that could move the score. One variable, not ten.
  4. Re-measure — with the same check.
  5. Keep only verified wins — keep the change only if the number objectively went up and nothing that worked before broke. Didn't move it, or regressed anything? Revert. No "probably better."
  6. STOP for the human — when beating the score needs a real decision (a trade-off between two goods, a contradiction between sources, an irreversible or load-bearing change), don't force it: log it, ask, move to the next-highest improvement. Climb until you can't beat the score without a human.

    Why it's cool

    Most "self-improving" prompts are a vibe: they claim they got better and you have to believe them. This one cannot, by construction. The score isn't invented — it's discovered in the target's own ground truth, so the loop can't grade its own homework. Pointed at code the score becomes tests; at a design, a screenshot; at prose, a rubric; at the RAPP ecosystem, real drift numbers from its own verifiers (that's rapp-loop.prompt.md, the rock-tumbler — same spine, score = drift count + mirror match + enum coverage + parity). Point that same spine at loop.prompt.md and the score becomes: does a throwaway target still converge to drift 0 under this wording, with no rule broken?

    So the file that defines the ratchet gets tightened only where a re-runnable check says it got tighter — and, most of the honest part, it stops itself at the edits that are genuinely a human's call (is the literal 120 better than a percentage? that's a trade-off between concreteness and generality — not the loop's to decide). A prompt rewrote the rules of prompt-rewriting, kept only what it could re-prove, and told you exactly where it wasn't allowed to decide for you.

    Run it yourself

    Open loop.prompt.md, and point its own /loop block at the file it lives in. Paste this to your agent:

    /loop Improve whatever you've been pointed at — and what you've been pointed at is THIS
file, loop.prompt.md, the ratchet itself.

FIRST PASS — establish the game:
  WHAT:   loop.prompt.md, the generic improvement ratchet.
  BETTER: (a) a fresh reader runs ONE correct pass from it alone,
          (b) it STOPS at real human-decision boundaries instead of forcing them,
          (c) the score-discovery step is unambiguous.
  SCORE:  paste the candidate wording over a THROWAWAY target (a tiny messy doc, a
          failing test) and score a 5-point checklist — did it (1) name a real score,
          (2) make exactly one change, (3) re-measure, (4) keep only the verified win,
          (5) halt at a genuine human call? "Converged" = the throwaway reaches its own
          drift 0 with no rule broken. That checklist is your 100.

EACH PASS: measure the checklist first · change ONE clause · re-measure with the SAME
throwaway · keep only if it objectively went up and nothing regressed, else REVERT · log
one line. STOP and ask when beating the score needs a real decision; say exactly where.

    Related: rapp-loop.prompt.md is the same loop pointed at the RAPP ecosystem, scored by its own drift tools — the "real drift verifiers" this pattern borrows.
