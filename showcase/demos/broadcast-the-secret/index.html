      Why it's cool

      Every other "private" thing you own is private because of where it lives — behind a login, on a server, in an app that can be shut down, breached, or told to hand it over. This one is private because of a seal, not a place. The ciphertext is math: publish it to eight billion people and it still reveals nothing — not the secret, not its length beyond a few bytes, not even whether you changed it between two seals (fresh random salt + nonce every time, so the same secret encrypts to totally different bytes).

      That flips the economics of a dead-man's switch. You don't pay a service to hold your secret until you're gone; you publish the sealed file everywhere now — cheap, redundant, un-deletable — and the only thing that has to survive is the key: a passphrase in a will, a QR on paper in a safe-deposit box, a line memorized by one person. The day the key surfaces, the secret opens. Until then it's immortal and invisible, with nothing left running and nothing left to pay for. That refusal you can trigger above is the confidentiality twin of /fn's verify-before-exec: integrity proves the bytes weren't changed; the seal proves only a key-holder can read them. Two independent planes, both enforced in your own browser, both server-free.

      Run it yourself

      1. Type a real secret and a passphrase in ①, hit Seal & publish, and copy the base64 that appears — that string is safe to post literally anywhere.
      2. In ②, keep the auto-filled ciphertext, type the passphrase, and Unseal. Then flip one character of the passphrase and watch the seal refuse.
      3. The envelope is a tiny self-describing format — magic RSL1 · 16-byte salt · 12-byte nonce · AES-256-GCM ciphertext+tag — so any RSL1 reader (browser, agent, CLI) can open it with the key alone.

      Local: python3 -m http.server in the repo root, then open localhost:8000/showcase/demos/broadcast-the-secret/ (crypto.subtle needs a secure context — localhost or https, not file://).

      For agents 🤖

      Reusable pattern:

      Compose it: publish an rapp-sealed/1.0 envelope to any fully-public, permanent store (a repo, a content-store, IPFS) and gate reads by key-possession alone — confidentiality as a seal, not a location. It sits orthogonal to /fn verify-before-exec (integrity): a payload can be sealed and hash-pinned, and either check can refuse independently, both in the client.
