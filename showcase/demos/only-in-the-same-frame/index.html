Only in the Same Frame

① Live — split a secret across two objects

No server, no upload. Your browser rolls a random 256-bit AES key, seals the message with AES-256-GCM, then XOR-splits the key into two shares. Object A carries the ciphertext + share A; Object B carries share B — nothing else. Print each as a QR and mail them to opposite ends of the earth. Neither square contains the words; neither contains the key.

        Why it's cool

        Every "secret" you own has an address — a server, a login, a vault, a keyring — a place that can be photographed, breached, or subpoenaed. This one has no address at all. Split the key with XOR and each share is a perfect one-time pad over the other: given Object A, all 2256 keys are exactly equally likely, so the plaintext carries zero information — not encrypted-and-guessable, genuinely absent. The message does not live in Object A, does not live in Object B, and never lived on any server, because no server ever held a whole key or watched the two meet.

        Distinct from a single-key dead-man's switch (where the ciphertext is one guessable passphrase away): here neither half is ever sufficient, so the words have no location in space until two physical objects share one camera frame. And the reassembly rides two independent, browser-enforced planes at once — confidentiality (only shareA ⊕ shareB reads the ciphertext) and integrity (the /fn cell that does the reassembly refuses to run unless its own bytes hash to the pin). Flip one byte of the cell with 🧪 Tamper above and verify-before-exec says no — no host did; your own browser did.

        Run it yourself

        1. Type a real secret in ① and hit Split. Copy the two base64 blobs — Object A (ciphertext + share A) and Object B (share B). Each is safe to print, mail, or post on its own; together they are the secret.
        2. Encode each blob as a QR (the repo's zero-dependency /track encoder does this) and put them on two different carriers — a coffee mug, a keyring, two people. Read one QR into a scan page, then the other; the join happens only in that browser.
        3. In ③, keep both auto-filled objects and press Bring them into one frame to run the pinned reframe cell live. Then press 🧪 Tamper to watch verify-before-exec refuse a byte-flipped cell.

        Local: python3 -m http.server in the repo root, then open localhost:8000/showcase/demos/only-in-the-same-frame/ (crypto.subtle needs a secure context — localhost or https, not file://).

        For agents 🤖

        Reusable pattern:

        Compose it: XOR-split any rapp-sealed key into an n-of-n set of shares, distribute one share per carrier (QR, agent, device, jurisdiction), and reassemble+decrypt inside a hash-pinned /fn cell. Confidentiality (secret-sharing) and integrity (verify-before-exec) are orthogonal planes: either can refuse independently, both in the client, with no key or plaintext ever touching a server.
